The Ghetto

Who makes a neighborhood ghetto?

Homeowners, landlords, and tenants alike point to those neglecting upkeep of their property as a major reason why a neighborhood becomes a ghetto.

Many do their fair share to maintain the character of the neighborhood. They believe it’s unfair that others are not accepting their neighborly responsibility to maintain their property.

Residents also believe the city must contribute their fair share of maintenance and improvements in the South of Downtown neighborhood. As an owner of the largest portfolios of property, the city has a large stake in the decline of South of Downtown into a ghetto.

A typical reason for the lack in public investment is a lack of money.

A common prescription for this largely working-class neighborhood is to entice higher income residents to move into their neighborhood, thus creating a more “mixed-income” neighborhood. Another prescription is to attract private investment for new developments with the expectation that it will generate the tax revenue required to justify public investments in the neighborhood.

Will working-class neighborhoods ever generate enough money to warrant improvements? Must they depend on outside investors to justify public investments?

How bad of a public investment is South of Downtown?

2nd Highest

Property Tax Contributor

When it comes to property taxes, South of Downtown is a top performer in Lincoln. At $32,247 per acre, South of Downtown is the highest contributor to property taxes after #1 ranked Downtown. That’s 4 times more than all the Walmarts in Lincoln combined.

#1 Ranked

Wheel Tax Contributor

When it comes to wheel taxes, South of Downtown out performs every neighborhood in Lincoln. At $1,342 per acre, South of Downtown contributes more to wheel taxes than any other neighborhood in Lincoln. It yields twice as much as the #2 contributor and 4 times as much as Downtown.

It’s the best public investment in town.

Where’s the residents’ return on investment?

If looking for mega-projects as evidence of private investment, none will be found in the South of Downtown neighborhood. It’s already built out and inhabited by many residents. Mega-projects won’t be popping up like they will in Lincoln’s less inhabited Telegraph, Railyard, and South Haymarket districts.

Despite the lack of mega-projects, private investments are still being made South of Downtown.

Many small private investments are being made by residents, homeowners, landlords, and small businesses.

They’re starting new businesses, altering interiors to better serve customers, creating new entertainment events and festivals, restoring older buildings to preserve their historic character, improving private properties to provide community-owned public space, and making many other small to moderate investments that are within their means.

These small private investments may not warrant large public mega-projects like mid-rise parking garages and tourist-attracting stadiums. But, just like the small private investments in South of Downtown,

small public investments add up to large community impact.
